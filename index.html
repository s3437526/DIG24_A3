Dementia affects thinking, behaviour and ability to perform everyday tasks
It is not a normal part of ageing process
Brain function affected enough to interfere with normal social and working life

4 MAIN CAUSES
TOP

Alzheimer’s disease is the most common cause of dementia, comprising between 60 to 80% of all cases of dementia. It is more common in women than men.


The pathology underpinning Alzheimer’s disease is believed to derive from the accumulation of beta-amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles. These first induce neuronal injury and subsequently result in neuronal death. The resulting decrease in cholinergic neurotransmission results in the loss of memory and cognitive function.


While the onset of Alzheimer’s disease is commonly undetected, the first signs are usually short-term memory loss. This is followed by gradual deficits in cognitive function over time, affecting multiple cognitive domains. These include memory loss such as poor recall or losing items; aphasia; agnosia; apraxia; disorientation (i.e. impairments in time perception and inability to recognise familiar people); and impaired visuospatial function and executive function.


Alzheimer’s disease is usually characterised by early problems in memory and visuospatial abilities such as getting lost in familiar environments. People living with Alzheimer’s disease also commonly experience non-cognitive symptoms (also referred to an neuropsychiatric or behavioural symptoms) during their illness.


A diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease can only be confirmed via a post-mortem examination.

Vascular disease is the second most common cause of dementia after Alzheimer’s disease and accounts for around 15-20% of all cases of dementia. It is most common in men over the age of 70.


Vascular dementia is caused by neuronal deprivation of oxygen as a result of conditions that block or reduce blood flow to the brain. Stoke is the most common cause of such deprivation and Vascular dementia. However, not everyone who has a stroke will progress to Vascular dementia (about 20% of those who have a stroke will develop dementia).


The duration of Vascular dementia varies greatly from person to person. Progression of Vascular dementia is often stepwise which means that symptoms stabilise after the initial damage but if there is another vascular event (i.e. stroke), the symptoms increase and the person’s condition deteriorates further.


However, progression may appear to be continuous for those with multiple Transient Ischemic Attacks (or mini strokes). The decline appears gradual, because the damage is small, but ongoing.


In later stages of Vascular dementia, the person becomes increasingly frail and may lose awareness of their surroundings. On average, death from Vascular dementia occurs earlier that than it does for someone with Alzheimer’s disease (usually within 5 years from the onset of symptoms). Death is usually the result of a stroke or heart attack resulting from the underlying vascular disease.

Lewy Body dementia is the third most common cause of dementia. 10 to 25% of diagnoses of dementia are for Lewy Body dementia. Most people diagnosed with dementia with lewy bodies are over the age of 60 years.


Lewy Body dementia occurs as a result of abnormal folding of the alpha-synuclein protein. Physical rigidity commonly occurs early in the progression of the disease and is characterised by a shuffling gait and difficulty “getting going”. Tremors generally occur later in the illness but in other cases may be present before other symptoms.


Brain damage associated with Lewy Body dementia starts in the midbrain (the substantia nigra in the basal ganglia) which controls movement. This accounts for problems initiating movement and the slow, effortful gait (i.e. slow shuffled walking).


Lewy Body dementia also affects the brainstem, which is instrumental in attention and sleep. This accounts for the commonly observed sleep disturbances associated with Lewy Body dementia. Individuals with Lewy Body dementia also often experience hallucinations and delusions, which has led these to be considered defining symptoms of Lewy Body dementia.


In Lewy Body dementia individuals progressively experience problems with attention and executive functioning (i.e. problems with decision making, planning, and thinking). While early symptoms are similar to early stages of other types of dementia, Lewy Body dementia can be differentiated from other types of dementia because short-term memory is commonly preserved and memory problems are often not present until later in the disease.


Lewy Body dementia is also characterised by fluctuating levels of cognitive function with periods of alertness and coherence that alternate with unresponsiveness and confusion. In other words, a person with Lewy Body dementia may be alert and functioning well and then suddenly be very confused. Such states of confusion may last for hours or even days. This can be confused with delirium.

Frontotemporal dementia affects the frontal and temporal lobes of the brain and accounts for up to 10% of all cases of dementia. Frontotemporal dementia is the second most common cause of younger onset dementia (after Alzheimer's disease).


Frontotemporal dementia can be hereditary, but there are many non-hereditary cases as well. Age of onset is typically younger (55 to 65 years) and it is one of the types of dementia categorised as younger onset.


As with Alzheimer’s disease, Frontotemporal dementia is characterised by mutations of proteins in the frontal and temporal lobes of the brain. In Frontotemporal dementia, Tau and TDP-43 proteins which help maintain the structure of brain cells and transport nutrients within cells mutate, leading to clumping. This clumping means the protein is unable to perform its function and results in cell death.


In some cases, Frontotemporal dementia will have genetic origins. In approximately one third of cases diagnosed the patient has a close relative with the disease, usually across generations. Apart from this genetic connection, there has been no specific cause identified that explains the cases where familial connections cannot be identified.


Symptom presentation of Frontotemporal dementia differs depending on the specific area of the brain affected by the disease. However, Frontotemporal dementia predominantly affects personality and language abilities and is characterised by behaviour becoming disinhibited and repetitive. Unlike Alzheimer’s disease, memory loss is uncommon in the early stages of Frontotemporal dementia.

TOP
There are a number of things we can do to help reduce the risk of developing dementia. Some of these things include:

Cardiovascular Risk Factors such as high blood pressure, high body weight and high cholesterol in mid-life have all been linked to a higher risk of developing dementia.


However, these risk factors are modifiable, and if managed correctly during mid-life can help reduce risk of developing dementia.


The reason why we talk about the importance of vascular risk factors for dementia is because the brain consists of many blood vessels. This is why it is said that a ‘healthy heart gives you a healthy brain’ as anything that affects the blood vessels in the body/heart will affect blood vessels in the brain. This is why we need to take care of your blood vessels.
